### Exercise Categories

**1. Conceptual Foundations**
*   **Description**: These exercises test the understanding of fundamental definitions, core principles, and basic properties of logical concepts. They typically require recalling knowledge rather than complex problem-solving.
*   **Example**: EX 1.1.1 (What is a valid propositional sentence?)

**2. Logical Inference & Application**
*   **Description**: Exercises in this category require applying specific logical rules (e.g., truth tables, rules of implication) to a given scenario to infer conclusions or determine possible outcomes. They focus on understanding how logical operators function in practice.
*   **Example**: EX 1.1.2 (If P → Q is true and P is false, then Q can be...)

**3. Expression Manipulation & Equivalence**
*   **Description**: This category involves transforming, simplifying, or comparing logical expressions using established equivalence laws and rules (e.g., De Morgan's Laws, conditional equivalence). The goal is to demonstrate proficiency in altering the form of an expression while preserving its logical truth value.
*   **Examples**: EX 1.1.3 (Which of the following expressions is logically equivalent to...), EX 1.1.4 (What is the negation of the proposition P ↔ Q?)

**4. Deductive Reasoning & Problem Solving**
*   **Description**: These exercises present multi-step problems that require structured logical deduction. Learners must analyze given conditions, infer intermediate truth values, and then use these inferences to determine the truth values or properties of other propositions.
*   **Example**: EX 1.1.5 (Knowing that the truth value of P→Q is 0, what are the truth values of PΛQ and Q→P?)
